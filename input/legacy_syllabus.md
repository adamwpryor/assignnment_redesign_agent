# Assignment Overview

This assignment invites students to examine how classical rhetorical
principles—ethos, pathos, and logos—continue to shape communication across
contemporary media landscapes. Through close reading and critical analysis, you
will develop a nuanced understanding of persuasion as both an art and a social
force.

By the end of this assignment, you should be able to identify rhetorical
strategies embedded in everyday discourse, evaluate the ethical dimensions of
persuasive communication, and construct original arguments that demonstrate
sophisticated rhetorical awareness.

# Learning Objectives

Upon successful completion of this assignment, students will be able to:

Define and apply Aristotle's three modes of
persuasion—ethos, pathos, and logos—to contemporary texts.

Analyze how digital platforms and social media reshape
traditional rhetorical relationships between speaker, audience, and context.

Evaluate the ethical responsibilities of communicators
in an era of algorithmic amplification and misinformation.

Compose a rhetorically self-aware analytical essay that
demonstrates mastery of audience, purpose, and context.

# Background Reading and Conceptual Framework

## The Classical Tradition

Rhetoric, broadly defined, is the art of effective or persuasive
communication. Though frequently dismissed in popular usage as mere
manipulation or empty speech, the rhetorical tradition represents one of the
oldest and most sophisticated disciplines in Western intellectual history.
Aristotle's Rhetoric (circa 350 BCE) identified three primary appeals—ethos
(credibility), pathos (emotional resonance), and logos (logical reasoning)—as
the foundational tools through which speakers move audiences toward belief or
action.

Ethos refers to the character and credibility a speaker projects, both
through demonstrated expertise and through the ethical integrity implied by
their choices of argument. Pathos engages the emotional lives of audiences,
acknowledging that human decision-making is never purely rational. Logos
constructs logical arguments through evidence, reasoning, and structured
claims.

Crucially, Aristotle understood these appeals as context-dependent. The
rhetorical situation—comprising the speaker, audience, subject, occasion, and
purpose—determines which combination of appeals is appropriate and effective.
Rhetoric is not a fixed formula but a dynamic, adaptive practice.

## Rhetoric in Digital Contexts

Contemporary communication scholars have extended classical rhetorical
theory to address the dramatically transformed conditions of digital media.
Carolyn Miller's concept of genre as social action, for example, has been
applied to emergent digital forms such as tweets, memes, and viral video
essays—each of which carries genre conventions that shape audience expectations
and communicative possibilities.

Digital rhetoric introduces new complexities that classical frameworks
did not anticipate. The collapse of clear boundaries between author and
audience, the acceleration of information circulation, the algorithmic curation
of content, and the persistence of digital texts all alter the rhetorical
dynamics through which persuasion operates. A political advertisement on social
media does not simply present arguments to a passive audience; it is shaped by
platform affordances, targeted by demographic data, and remixed by other users
who share, critique, or parody it.

Scholars such as Ian Bogost have also examined procedural rhetoric—the
persuasive logic embedded in interactive media and video games—expanding the
field beyond verbal and visual communication. This widening of the rhetorical
lens reflects an important truth: rhetoric is not confined to speeches and
essays. It inheres in any communicative act that seeks to influence belief,
attitude, identity, or behavior.

## Ethics and the Rhetorical Situation

The question of ethics has always been central to rhetorical theory,
though often contested. Plato's Gorgias famously indicted rhetoric as a form of
flattery that produces mere belief rather than genuine knowledge. In contrast,
Aristotle argued that rhetoric is ethically neutral—a tool that may serve good
or bad ends depending on the character of the user. Contemporary scholars
largely reject both extremes, arguing instead that rhetorical choices always
carry ethical implications that demand critical scrutiny.

In digital environments, these ethical stakes are amplified. The rise of
computational propaganda, astroturfing campaigns, and AI-generated
disinformation challenges the classical assumption of a human speaker operating
in good faith. Simultaneously, marginalized communities have leveraged digital
rhetoric to build counterpublics, organize collective action, and challenge
dominant narratives—demonstrating rhetoric's capacity to disrupt as well as to
reinforce existing power structures.

# Assignment Prompt

Select one contemporary communicative text for close rhetorical analysis.
Your chosen text may be a political speech, a viral social media campaign, an
op-ed, an advertising campaign, a podcast segment, or a public health
communication effort from the past five years. The text must be publicly
available and of sufficient complexity to sustain sustained analysis.

In an essay of 2,000–2,500 words, perform a rhetorical analysis of your
chosen text. Your essay should address the following:

•
Rhetorical Situation: Describe the context in which the
text was produced and received. Who is the author or organization? Who
constitutes the target audience? What occasion or exigence prompted this
communication? What constraints shaped it?

•
Appeals Analysis: Identify and analyze the use of
ethos, pathos, and logos within the text. Provide specific textual evidence.
Where one appeal is notably absent, consider what that absence communicates.

•
Medium and Platform: Analyze how the medium and
platform shape the rhetoric. How do the affordances of the chosen channel
amplify, constrain, or transform the persuasive message?

•
Ethical Evaluation: Assess the ethical dimensions of
the text's rhetorical strategies. Does the text employ deceptive framing,
selective omission, or emotional manipulation? Conversely, does it model
transparent, audience-respecting communication? Engage with at least one
ethical framework from course readings.

•
Effectiveness: Evaluate the text's rhetorical
effectiveness. Does the deployment of appeals suit the audience, occasion, and
purpose? What evidence—if any—exists for its real-world impact?

Your essay must integrate at least five scholarly sources, including a
minimum of two peer-reviewed articles from communication journals. Sources
should be cited using APA 7th edition format throughout.

# Sample Analytical Passage (Modeling the Approach)

The following passage illustrates the kind of close rhetorical reading
this assignment invites. Note how the writer moves between specific textual
evidence and broader theoretical claims.

The 2020 public health campaign 'We Can Do
This,' launched by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, deploys a
carefully calibrated combination of all three Aristotelian appeals to promote
COVID-19 vaccination. Its ethos is constructed not through celebrity
endorsement—notably absent in early iterations—but through the visual language
of institutional authority: physicians in clinical settings, public health
officials delivering measured, evidence-based statements. This choice reflects
an awareness of the campaign's rhetorical situation: a deeply polarized
audience whose trust in media figures had eroded, but who retained—in certain
demographics—residual trust in medical professionals. The campaign's pathos
operates subtly, favoring images of reconnection with elderly family members
over fear-based messaging about death and hospitalization. This tonal choice
suggests a rhetorical judgment that positive emotional appeals would be more
persuasive than threat-based ones for a vaccine-hesitant audience fatigued by
alarming news. Finally, the logos of the campaign is embedded in its data
visualizations and direct citation of clinical trial efficacy rates—a choice
that acknowledges, perhaps optimistically, that a portion of its audience
remains open to empirical persuasion.

Observe that this passage does not merely identify the three appeals but
analyzes the reasoning behind their deployment, connecting rhetorical choices
to audience awareness and communicative purpose. Your essay should model this
analytical depth throughout.

# Evaluation Criteria and Grading Rubric

This assignment is worth 25% of your final course grade (100 points
total). Submissions will be evaluated according to the following criteria:

Analytical Rigor (30 points): Does the essay move
beyond surface-level description to offer genuinely interpretive claims? Is
textual evidence selected and deployed precisely? Are analytical claims
sufficiently specific and defensible?

Theoretical Engagement (25 points): Does the essay
demonstrate a command of relevant rhetorical concepts and frameworks? Are
theoretical terms used accurately and with nuance rather than as labels? Is
course material genuinely synthesized rather than superficially cited?

Argumentation and Structure (20 points): Does the essay
advance a coherent, debatable thesis? Is the argument developed progressively
across well-organized paragraphs? Are transitions clear and purposeful?

Research and Citation (15 points): Are sources
appropriate, credible, and meaningfully integrated? Does the essay demonstrate
genuine engagement with scholarly literature rather than decorative citation?
Are all sources correctly cited in APA 7th edition format?

Writing Quality (10 points): Is the prose clear,
precise, and appropriately formal for academic writing? Are grammatical errors,
unclear antecedents, and hedging qualifiers avoided? Does the essay demonstrate
control over sentence structure and register?

# Submission Guidelines

Submit your completed essay through the course learning management system
by 11:59 PM on the due date indicated in the course schedule. Late submissions
will be penalized 5% per day unless an extension has been arranged in advance

Format requirements: double-spaced, 12-point Times New Roman font,
one-inch margins on all sides, APA 7th edition title page and reference list

be submitted as a .docx or .pdf file.

writing assistants as part of their research and drafting process, provided

own, and that any AI-assisted text is substantially revised and integrated into
an original intellectual framework. You are expected to be able to discuss and
defend your analysis in class. Uncritical reliance on AI-generated text that
substitutes for genuine engagement with the assignment will be treated as an
academic integrity concern.
